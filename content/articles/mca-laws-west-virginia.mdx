West Virginia does not have laws that specifically regulate merchant cash advances. The state's Division of Financial Institutions oversees banks and licensed lenders, but MCAs are structured as purchases of future receivables rather than loans, which places them outside the scope of West Virginia's lending regulations.

This guide explains how existing West Virginia laws apply to MCAs and what business owners should know to protect themselves.

<h2 id="current-mca-regulations">Current MCA Regulations in West Virginia</h2>

West Virginia has not enacted any legislation targeting MCAs directly. The Division of Financial Institutions regulates traditional lenders, credit unions, and other financial institutions, but MCA companies are generally not required to obtain a license or register with the state.

Because MCAs are classified as commercial transactions rather than loans, they are not subject to West Virginia's usury statute, which sets limits on interest rates for certain types of loans. This means MCA companies can charge factor rates that translate to very high APR equivalents without violating state law.

<h3 id="no-disclosure-requirements">No Disclosure Requirements</h3>

West Virginia does not require MCA companies to provide standardized cost disclosures. There is no state mandate for showing an APR equivalent, total cost of financing, or a fee breakdown. Business owners in West Virginia must do their own analysis to understand the true cost of an MCA.

Before signing any MCA agreement, request the total repayment amount, the factor rate, all fees, and the expected repayment timeline. Use this information to calculate an APR equivalent so you can compare with other financing options.

<h2 id="confession-of-judgment-rules">Confession of Judgment Rules</h2>

West Virginia has protections against confessions of judgment. West Virginia courts generally require proper notice and due process before a judgment can be entered against a defendant. Cognovit notes are viewed with disfavor, and courts will scrutinize whether the debtor knowingly and voluntarily waived their rights.

If an MCA company obtains a COJ judgment against your West Virginia business in another state, enforcing that judgment in West Virginia would require domestication through West Virginia courts. You would have the opportunity to challenge the judgment at that stage, and West Virginia courts would evaluate whether proper due process was provided.

If you encounter a COJ clause in an MCA contract, consult a West Virginia attorney before signing. While the state provides some protection, it is always better to understand the risks upfront.

<h2 id="ucc-filing-rules">UCC Filing Rules</h2>

MCA companies file UCC-1 financing statements with the West Virginia Secretary of State to establish their interest in your business's future receivables. These filings are public records that other lenders and creditors can see.

Key points about UCC filings in West Virginia:

- You can search for UCC filings against your business through the [West Virginia Secretary of State](https://www.wvsos.com/) website
- UCC filings are effective for five years from the date of filing
- After paying off an MCA, the funder must file a UCC-3 termination statement within 20 business days of receiving your written demand
- If the funder does not terminate the filing, you may have a claim for damages under West Virginia's version of the Uniform Commercial Code

Multiple UCC filings from different MCA companies can make it very difficult to obtain traditional bank financing. Lenders see multiple active liens as a sign of financial distress. Check your filings regularly and make sure paid-off MCAs are properly released.

<h2 id="consumer-protection-laws">Consumer Protection Laws That Apply</h2>

West Virginia's Consumer Credit and Protection Act (WV Code Chapter 46A) provides protections against unfair and deceptive business practices. While this statute is primarily aimed at consumer transactions, certain provisions may apply to commercial dealings, particularly when a sole proprietor is involved.

<h3 id="ag-enforcement">Attorney General Enforcement</h3>

The West Virginia Attorney General's Consumer Protection Division investigates complaints about unfair or deceptive business practices. If an MCA company uses misleading marketing, misrepresents the cost of financing, hides fees, or engages in aggressive collection tactics, you can file a complaint with the Attorney General.

<h3 id="dfi-oversight">Division of Financial Institutions</h3>

The Division of Financial Institutions can provide guidance about financial companies operating in West Virginia. If you believe an MCA company is engaging in practices that violate state law, contacting the Division is a useful step even though MCA companies may not be directly regulated by it.

<h2 id="recent-legislation">Recent Legislation and Court Cases</h2>

- **No pending MCA legislation.** West Virginia has not introduced any bills to regulate MCAs or require commercial financing disclosures as of early 2026.
- **Usury considerations.** If a West Virginia court determines that a specific MCA agreement is actually a loan (because it has fixed payments, no reconciliation, and a guaranteed return to the funder), the state's usury limits could potentially apply.
- **National enforcement.** FTC enforcement actions against MCA companies for deceptive practices apply nationwide, including in West Virginia.
- **Small business impact.** West Virginia's small business community relies on access to capital, and as MCA use grows, there is increasing potential for legislative attention.

<h2 id="what-business-owners-should-do">What West Virginia Business Owners Should Do</h2>

If you are considering an MCA in West Virginia or already have one, take these steps:

1. **Request a full cost breakdown.** Since West Virginia has no disclosure requirements, you need to ask the MCA company for the total repayment amount, factor rate, all fees, and an APR estimate. Calculate the true cost yourself and compare it with other financing options.
2. **Watch for COJ clauses.** West Virginia courts disfavor cognovit notes, but you should still carefully review your contract for confession of judgment provisions. If you find one, discuss it with an attorney before signing.
3. **Check your UCC filings.** Visit the [West Virginia Secretary of State](https://www.wvsos.com/) website to search for active UCC liens on your business. Make sure old liens from paid-off MCAs have been terminated.
4. **Verify reconciliation terms.** A genuine MCA should adjust payments when your revenue drops. If the contract requires fixed daily payments regardless of your actual sales, the agreement may be a loan in disguise, which could give you additional legal protections.
5. **Explore alternatives.** Contact the [West Virginia Small Business Development Center](https://www.wvsbdc.com/) for free counseling on SBA loans, business lines of credit, and other financing options that are typically less expensive than an MCA.
