Wisconsin does not have laws that specifically regulate merchant cash advances. The state's Department of Financial Institutions (DFI) oversees banks, credit unions, and licensed lenders, but MCAs are structured as purchases of future receivables rather than loans, which puts them outside the scope of Wisconsin's lending regulations.

This guide explains how existing Wisconsin laws apply to MCAs and what business owners should do to protect their interests.

<h2 id="current-mca-regulations">Current MCA Regulations in Wisconsin</h2>

Wisconsin has not passed any legislation specifically targeting MCAs. The Department of Financial Institutions licenses and regulates traditional lenders, but MCA companies are generally not required to hold a license in Wisconsin.

Because MCAs are classified as commercial transactions rather than loans, they are not subject to Wisconsin's usury statute. Wisconsin law caps interest rates on certain types of loans, but those limits do not apply to MCA transactions. This means MCA companies can charge factor rates that, when converted to an APR, can be significantly higher than what a traditional lender could charge.

<h3 id="no-disclosure-requirements">No Disclosure Requirements</h3>

Wisconsin does not require MCA companies to provide standardized cost disclosures. There is no state mandate for MCA providers to present an APR, total cost of financing, or a detailed fee breakdown. Business owners must do their own research to understand the true cost of an MCA.

Before signing, request the total repayment amount, the factor rate, all fees, and the expected repayment timeline from the MCA company. Then calculate the APR equivalent so you can make an informed comparison with other financing options.

<h2 id="confession-of-judgment-rules">Confession of Judgment Rules</h2>

Wisconsin has restrictions on confessions of judgment. Under Wisconsin law, cognovit judgments are generally disfavored, and courts require proper notice and an opportunity to be heard before a judgment can be enforced against a defendant.

If an MCA company obtains a COJ judgment against your Wisconsin business in another state (such as New York), enforcing that judgment in Wisconsin would require domestication through Wisconsin courts. You would have the opportunity to challenge the judgment at that stage, and Wisconsin courts would evaluate whether proper procedural protections were followed.

If you see a COJ clause in an MCA contract, take it seriously and consult a Wisconsin attorney before signing. While Wisconsin provides some protections, understanding your specific risks is important.

<h2 id="ucc-filing-rules">UCC Filing Rules</h2>

MCA companies file UCC-1 financing statements with the Wisconsin Department of Financial Institutions to establish their claim on your business's future receivables. These filings are public records.

What you need to know about UCC filings in Wisconsin:

- You can search for UCC filings against your business through the [Wisconsin DFI](https://www.wdfi.org/) website
- UCC filings in Wisconsin are effective for five years from the date of filing
- After you pay off an MCA, the funder must file a UCC-3 termination statement within 20 business days of receiving your written demand
- If the funder fails to terminate the filing after receiving your demand, you may have a claim for damages under Wisconsin's version of the Uniform Commercial Code

Multiple active UCC filings from different MCA companies can hurt your ability to secure traditional financing. Banks see multiple liens as a red flag. Check your filings regularly and make sure old MCAs are properly released.

<h2 id="consumer-protection-laws">Consumer Protection Laws That Apply</h2>

Wisconsin's Deceptive Trade Practices Act (Wisconsin Statutes Chapter 100) prohibits unfair methods of competition and unfair or deceptive acts in business dealings. While primarily aimed at consumer transactions, certain provisions can apply to commercial transactions.

<h3 id="datcp-complaints">DATCP Complaints</h3>

The Wisconsin Department of Agriculture, Trade and Consumer Protection (DATCP) handles complaints about deceptive business practices. If an MCA company uses misleading advertising, misrepresents the terms of an advance, hides fees, or uses aggressive collection tactics, you can file a complaint with DATCP.

<h3 id="private-claims">Private Claims</h3>

Wisconsin law allows private lawsuits for violations of the Deceptive Trade Practices Act. If an MCA company engaged in deceptive conduct that caused you financial harm, you may be able to pursue a legal claim. Consult a Wisconsin attorney to evaluate your options.

Additionally, federal laws such as the FTC Act and the Uniform Commercial Code (as adopted in Wisconsin) provide baseline protections for commercial transactions.

<h2 id="recent-legislation">Recent Legislation and Court Cases</h2>

- **No pending MCA legislation.** Wisconsin has not introduced any bills to regulate MCAs or require commercial financing disclosures as of early 2026.
- **Loan vs. MCA disputes.** Wisconsin courts have addressed disputes about whether certain MCA agreements are actually loans. If an MCA has fixed payments, no reconciliation, and a guaranteed return to the funder, a court could potentially reclassify it as a loan subject to Wisconsin's usury limits.
- **National trends.** Several states have passed commercial financing disclosure laws. While Wisconsin has not followed suit, growing MCA use in the state could lead to future legislative attention.
- **Federal enforcement.** FTC enforcement actions against MCA companies for deceptive practices apply in Wisconsin.

<h2 id="what-business-owners-should-do">What Wisconsin Business Owners Should Do</h2>

If you are considering an MCA in Wisconsin or already have one, take these steps:

1. **Calculate the true cost.** Since Wisconsin has no disclosure requirements, you need to figure out the APR equivalent yourself. Request the total repayment amount and the expected repayment period from the MCA company, then compare the cost to SBA loans, business lines of credit, and other options.
2. **Review the contract for COJ clauses.** Wisconsin restricts cognovit judgments, but you should still identify any confession of judgment provisions in the contract and discuss them with an attorney.
3. **Check your UCC filings.** Search the [Wisconsin DFI](https://www.wdfi.org/) website for active UCC liens on your business. Make sure old liens from paid-off MCAs have been properly terminated.
4. **Understand reconciliation.** A legitimate MCA adjusts payments based on your actual revenue. If your contract requires fixed daily payments regardless of how much you earn, the agreement may actually be a loan, which could give you additional legal protections under Wisconsin law.
5. **Get free counseling.** Contact the [Wisconsin Small Business Development Center](https://www.wisconsinsbdc.org/) for free advice on financing options. An SBDC counselor can help you evaluate whether an MCA is the right choice for your situation.
