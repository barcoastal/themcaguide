A merchant cash advance lawyer is an attorney who specializes in MCA contract disputes, helping business owners challenge abusive terms, defend against aggressive collection, or navigate the legal gray zone between a purchase-of-receivables agreement and a regulated loan.


## Quick Facts

- MCAs are structured as purchases of future receivables rather than loans, which has historically shielded providers from state usury laws in most jurisdictions.
- Factor rates typically range from 1.15 to 1.45, translating to estimated APRs of 40% to 350%+ depending on remittance speed, according to industry and academic analyses.
- New York banned out-of-state confession of judgment (COJ) clauses in 2019 following a Bloomberg investigation documenting widespread abuse against small businesses.
- The FTC filed suit against multiple MCA providers between 2020 and 2022, including RCG Advances and RAM Capital Funding, alleging deceptive marketing and collection practices.
- California's SB 1235 (effective January 2022) and New York's Commercial Finance Disclosure Law (effective August 2023) now require MCA providers to disclose estimated APR equivalents on covered transactions.
- The MCA industry advanced an estimated $19 billion in 2023, according to estimates cited by industry groups including the Small Business Finance Association.


## What Is a Merchant Cash Advance Lawyer?

A merchant cash advance (MCA) lawyer is an attorney who handles legal matters arising from MCA agreements. These lawyers typically work in one of two roles: defending business owners against funders who are collecting aggressively, or evaluating whether an MCA contract can be challenged, modified, or voided.

MCAs are not loans in the traditional legal sense. A funder purchases a percentage of your future receivables at a discount. Because of this structure, MCA providers have historically argued their products fall outside state usury laws and federal lending regulations. That argument has worked in many courts, but it is being challenged more frequently, which is why specialized legal help matters.

## When You Actually Need One

Not every MCA problem requires a lawyer. But several situations make legal counsel worth the cost.

**Confession of judgment (COJ) clauses.** Many MCA contracts include a COJ, a provision that lets the funder obtain a court judgment against you without filing a lawsuit first. You waive your right to contest the debt before it is entered. In 2019, New York banned out-of-state COJ clauses following a Bloomberg investigation that documented widespread abuse. If your MCA contract is governed by New York law, or another state that still permits COJs, you may have limited options once a judgment is filed.

**UCC liens and blocked financing.** Funders routinely file UCC-1 financing statements against your business assets. These can prevent you from obtaining other financing. If a funder files an unauthorized or inaccurate lien, or fails to terminate it after repayment, an attorney can challenge it.

**Stacking situations.** If you have multiple MCAs from different providers, each claiming remittance rights on the same receivables, you can hit a cash wall quickly. Lawyers experienced in MCA debt resolution sometimes negotiate structured payoffs that allow the business to survive.

**Aggressive collection or alleged breach.** If a funder declares you in default because revenue dropped and you are remitting less, their interpretation of the contract may not hold up legally. Default definitions in MCA contracts vary widely, and some are written so broadly that any revenue decline triggers a technical default even when you are paying exactly as the contract intended.

## The Legal Landscape: Numbers That Matter

The MCA industry moved roughly $19 billion in advances in 2023, according to estimates cited by industry groups including the Small Business Finance Association. That volume has drawn regulatory and enforcement attention.

**Factor rates vs. APR.** A typical MCA carries a factor rate between 1.15 and 1.45. On a $100,000 advance with a 1.35 factor rate, you repay $135,000 regardless of how quickly you pay it back. If you remit 15% of daily credit card sales and average $8,000 in daily revenue, you might pay it off in roughly 11 months, implying an APR around 63%. If your revenue slows, the repayment term extends but the total owed stays fixed. If revenue accelerates, you pay faster, and the effective APR climbs higher, sometimes past 150%.

**State disclosure laws.** California's SB 1235, effective January 1, 2022, requires commercial finance providers including MCA funders to disclose an estimated APR on transactions up to $500,000. New York's Commercial Finance Disclosure Law (CFDL), effective August 2023, applies similar requirements. Virginia and Utah have passed comparable legislation. An MCA lawyer familiar with your state's disclosure rules can identify whether your funder violated them, which may give you grounds to rescind or renegotiate.

**FTC enforcement.** The FTC filed suit against multiple MCA providers between 2020 and 2022, including RCG Advances and RAM Capital Funding, alleging deceptive marketing and collection practices. Some courts have found MCA agreements unconscionable or structured as de facto loans subject to usury analysis. The legal theory does not always succeed, but it has prevailed in enough cases to be worth exploring with counsel.

## How to Find the Right Lawyer

MCA law sits at the intersection of commercial finance, UCC Article 9, and state consumer protection statutes. Not every business attorney has litigated MCA cases. When evaluating candidates, ask specifically:

- Have you litigated MCA contracts, not just reviewed them?
- Are you familiar with the governing law in my contract (commonly New York or New Jersey)?
- Have you dealt with confessions of judgment in my state?
- Do you have experience negotiating with specific funders or their law firms?

Expect initial consultations to cost $200 to $500 per hour at small commercial litigation firms. Some MCA defense attorneys work on contingency for cases where funder conduct was particularly egregious. Others charge flat fees for document review, typically $500 to $1,500 for a contract analysis.

## Common Pitfalls

**Waiting too long.** If you stop remitting because you cannot afford payments, some funders move extremely fast. COJ clauses can result in a bank account freeze within days of a missed payment. If you see trouble coming, call an attorney before you miss a remittance, not after.

**Signing a second MCA to cover the first.** This is stacking, and it is one of the most common paths to insolvency. Each new funder files its own UCC lien and claims its own share of your receivables. The math compounds quickly. A lawyer can map out what each agreement actually costs and what total exposure you carry.

**Assuming the contract is unbreakable.** MCA contracts are written aggressively but they are not invincible. Courts have found problems with contracts that include fixed payment schedules (which undermine the purchase-of-receivables characterization), contracts with personal guarantees but no genuine reconciliation provision, and contracts marketed using materially false disclosures. A contract review can surface those issues.

**Ignoring the UCC filing after repayment.** After you repay an MCA in full, verify that the funder files a UCC-3 termination statement. Some do not. An active UCC-1 lien from an MCA you already paid can block future bank financing or SBA loans. If a lien remains past repayment, an attorney can send a demand letter or help you file a termination.

**Using a debt settlement company instead of a lawyer.** MCA-specific debt settlement firms are widely advertised online. Many are not law firms and cannot provide legal advice, appear in court, or negotiate agreements that are legally binding on the funder. If you are in a dispute involving a COJ or an active lawsuit, you need a licensed attorney.

## Action Steps

1. **Pull your MCA contracts now.** Read the governing law clause, the default definition, the reconciliation provision, and look for any mention of confession of judgment. Flag anything unclear before you are under pressure to act.

2. **Run your real cost.** Take the total repayment amount, subtract the advance, and divide by the advance amount. That is your cost of capital as a percentage. Estimate how many months repayment will take based on your average daily card volume. Have those numbers ready before any attorney consultation.

3. **Check your UCC filings.** Search your state's UCC registry (typically at the Secretary of State website) using your business legal name. List every active lien and which funder filed it.

4. **Get a contract review before signing another MCA.** If you are considering a new advance, a one-hour attorney review costs far less than unwinding a bad deal later. The review should cover factor rate, default triggers, COJ clauses, and remittance reconciliation terms.

5. **If you are already in default or being collected against, act today.** Ask any attorney you contact whether your contract contains a COJ, what state governs it, and whether the funder has already filed a judgment. Speed matters once collection begins.

An MCA lawyer will not make a bad advance disappear. But in many cases, they can slow collection, identify grounds for renegotiation, or challenge terms that cross legal lines. Against a six-figure MCA obligation, a few hours of specialized legal time is almost always worth the cost.
