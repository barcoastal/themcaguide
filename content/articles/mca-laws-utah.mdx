Utah has taken a notable step in MCA regulation by passing a commercial financing registration requirement. While the state has historically had a light regulatory touch on commercial transactions, the growth of the MCA industry has prompted lawmakers to create a framework for tracking and overseeing MCA companies operating in the state.

This guide covers what Utah law requires of MCA providers and what protections exist for Utah business owners.

<h2 id="current-mca-regulations">Current MCA Regulations in Utah</h2>

Utah's approach to MCA regulation centers on registration requirements rather than the disclosure-focused approach taken by states like California and New York. The state has passed legislation requiring commercial financing providers, including MCA companies, to register with the Utah Department of Financial Institutions (DFI).

<h3 id="registration-requirement">Commercial Financing Registration</h3>

Under Utah's commercial financing registration law, MCA companies must:

- **Register with the DFI.** MCA companies that do business with Utah businesses must file a registration with the Utah Department of Financial Institutions before offering or providing merchant cash advances in the state.
- **Provide basic company information.** The registration requires disclosure of the company's legal name, address, key personnel, and the types of commercial financing products offered.
- **Maintain the registration.** Companies must keep their registration current and update it when material information changes.
- **Comply with DFI oversight.** Registered companies are subject to examination and enforcement by the DFI.

This registration requirement is significant because it gives Utah regulators visibility into which MCA companies are operating in the state. Previously, MCA companies could operate freely in Utah without any state oversight. Now, the DFI has a list of active MCA providers and the authority to investigate complaints.

<h3 id="disclosure-status">Disclosure Requirements</h3>

Utah's registration law does not include the same detailed disclosure requirements found in California's SB 1235 or New York's Commercial Financing Disclosure Law. Utah does not currently require MCA companies to disclose APR, total cost of financing, or standardized payment terms.

However, the registration framework creates a foundation that Utah could build on with future disclosure requirements. Business owners should monitor the [Utah Legislature](https://le.utah.gov/) for proposed legislation that could add disclosure mandates.

<h2 id="confession-of-judgment-rules">Confession of Judgment Rules</h2>

Utah does not have a specific statute banning confessions of judgment in commercial transactions. However, Utah courts apply standard contract law principles that provide some protection:

- COJs must be voluntarily and knowingly signed
- Utah courts can review COJs for procedural fairness
- Courts may refuse to enforce a COJ if there is evidence of fraud, duress, or unconscionability
- Out-of-state COJ judgments must be domesticated through Utah courts, which provides an opportunity to challenge them

Utah's protections against COJs are moderate. not as strong as Texas (which bans them outright) but not nonexistent. If you are a Utah business owner who signed a COJ as part of an MCA agreement, consult with a Utah attorney about your options.

<h2 id="ucc-filing-rules">UCC Filing Rules</h2>

MCA companies file UCC-1 financing statements with the Utah Division of Corporations and Commercial Code. These filings create a public record of the lien on your business assets.

Key points for Utah business owners:

- Search for UCC filings through the [Utah Division of Corporations](https://corporations.utah.gov/) website
- Utah UCC filings are effective for five years
- Demand a UCC-3 termination statement in writing after satisfying your MCA obligation. The funder has 20 days to comply.
- Utah's Uniform Commercial Code provides remedies for wrongful failure to terminate a UCC filing

<h2 id="consumer-protection-laws">Consumer Protection Laws That Apply</h2>

<h3 id="ucpa">Utah Consumer Sales Practices Act</h3>

The Utah Consumer Sales Practices Act (Utah Code Section 13-11) prohibits deceptive and unconscionable business practices. While primarily a consumer protection statute, it may apply to certain MCA transactions, particularly when the business owner is unsophisticated and the MCA company engaged in deceptive conduct.

<h3 id="common-law">Common Law Protections</h3>

Utah courts apply standard contract law doctrines that can provide relief in MCA disputes:

- **Unconscionability.** Courts can refuse to enforce MCA agreements with terms so one-sided that they are oppressive.
- **Fraud.** If the MCA company made material misrepresentations about the terms or cost of the advance.
- **Duress.** If the business owner was pressured into the agreement under circumstances that left no reasonable alternative.

<h2 id="recent-legislation">Recent Legislation and Developments</h2>

Utah's MCA regulatory landscape is evolving:

- **Registration enforcement.** The DFI has begun enforcing registration requirements, and MCA companies operating without registration face penalties.
- **Industry growth.** Utah's growing small business sector has attracted increased MCA activity, which in turn has generated more complaints and regulatory attention.
- **National trends.** Utah lawmakers have been monitoring the disclosure laws passed in California, New York, and Virginia, and similar proposals have been discussed for Utah.
- **DFI examinations.** The DFI has the authority to examine registered MCA companies and has indicated it will use this authority to ensure compliance.

<h2 id="what-business-owners-should-do">What Utah Business Owners Should Do</h2>

1. **Verify the MCA company is registered.** Check with the [Utah DFI](https://dfi.utah.gov/) to confirm the MCA company is properly registered. An unregistered company is a red flag.
2. **Ask for full cost disclosure.** Even though Utah does not mandate it, insist on knowing the total payback amount, payment schedule, and effective APR before signing.
3. **Have an attorney review the agreement.** Given the limited regulatory protections in Utah, legal review is especially important.
4. **Search your UCC filings.** Check the [Utah Division of Corporations](https://corporations.utah.gov/) for existing liens before taking on MCA debt.
5. **Report problems.** If an MCA company is operating without registration or engaging in deceptive practices, report it to the [Utah DFI](https://dfi.utah.gov/).
