Bitty Advance occupies a specific niche in the merchant cash advance market: smaller advances to newer businesses that larger MCA funders will not touch. The firm is based in Sunrise, Florida, and has been active in the MCA space since the mid-2010s. Because Bitty accepts businesses with as little as 3 months of operation and relatively modest monthly deposits, the product fills a real gap for very new businesses. It also prices to match that risk. This review covers who Bitty actually is, what their rates and terms look like, who qualifies, and the alternatives worth comparing before signing.

We have not taken funding from Bitty Advance and have no commercial relationship with the firm. The review is based on public marketing materials, broker placement data, and customer reviews on public platforms.

<h2 id="who-bitty-is">Who Bitty Advance Is</h2>

Bitty Advance operates from Sunrise, Florida. The firm was founded in the mid-2010s and positions itself as a direct MCA funder focused on underwriting businesses that larger funders decline. Key structural facts to confirm through primary sources before any engagement:

- **Legal entity.** Confirm the exact legal name through the Florida Division of Corporations [Sunbiz search](https://search.sunbiz.org/). Match formation date against any "decade of experience" or similar marketing claim.
- **State licensing.** MCA products are not federally regulated as loans, but some states require commercial financing disclosure registrations. California, New York, Utah, Virginia, and other states have commercial financing disclosure regimes. Confirm Bitty's registration status in your state.
- **Parent or affiliate structure.** Some MCA brands operate under holding companies that own multiple MCA entities. Confirm the relationship through public corporate records and disclosures.

Bitty markets direct-to-small-business and also accepts submissions through the MCA broker network. If you received a pitch from a broker about Bitty specifically, the broker is typically paid a commission by Bitty on successful fundings. That commission becomes part of the cost of your advance whether or not the fee is explicitly disclosed to you. Our [MCA broker vs direct funder](/articles/mca-broker-vs-direct-funder) guide breaks down how this works.

For the broader MCA landscape the firm operates in, the [MCA debt relief 2026 guide](/guides/mca-debt-relief-2026-guide) is the orientation document.

<h2 id="what-they-offer">What They Offer</h2>

Based on public marketing and broker placement data, Bitty Advance's product set looks like this:

- **Advance amounts.** The bulk of Bitty fundings sit in the $2,000 to $25,000 range. Some deals skew smaller, a few up to around $50,000. Bitty is not the funder to approach for a $200,000 advance, both because the product is sized for smaller deals and because the underwriting cost math works against larger deals at these factor rates.
- **Factor rates.** Public reports and broker discussions place Bitty's factor rates typically in the 1.35 to 1.50 range. At 1.40 on a 90-day term, the effective APR is in the neighborhood of 250 percent. Use our [factor rate to APR calculator](/tools/factor-rate-to-apr) to run your specific quote.
- **Repayment structure.** Daily or weekly ACH debit against the business operating account. Repayment continues until the factor-rate total is paid.
- **Term length.** Most Bitty deals run 60 to 180 days. Shorter terms compress the daily payment; longer terms reduce each debit but extend the total cost burden.
- **Renewals and stacking.** Like most MCA funders, Bitty offers renewals when a borrower has paid down to roughly 50 percent of the original balance. Renewal structure is where MCA stacking often begins. Our [MCA stacking risks](/articles/mca-stacking-risks) guide explains the downstream damage.

Whatever your specific quote, convert it to a total dollar cost and effective APR before signing. A 1.40 factor on a $15,000 advance means $21,000 repaid, $6,000 in total fees over the term.

<h2 id="eligibility">Eligibility: Who Actually Qualifies</h2>

Bitty's qualifying thresholds are materially lower than larger MCA funders. The tradeoff for lower thresholds is higher factor rates. The typical eligibility profile:

- **Time in business.** 3 to 6 months is often sufficient. A handful of MCA funders will touch businesses this young; most require 6 to 12 months minimum.
- **Monthly deposits.** Reports indicate $10,000 to $12,000 in monthly business deposits can qualify. Larger MCA funders typically require $20,000 to $25,000 minimum.
- **Credit score.** Bitty will lend to business owners with credit scores in the 500 to 600 range, and sometimes below. Factor rates climb with lower scores. Businesses where the owner has 650+ credit often qualify for better options elsewhere.
- **Bank statements.** The standard MCA underwriting document set: three to six months of business bank statements showing deposit consistency and reasonable NSF activity. Excessive NSF will either disqualify the deal or push the factor rate higher.
- **Industry.** Most industries accepted except a restricted list (adult, cannabis in some states, unregulated gambling, certain financial services). Confirm industry eligibility before submitting.

If you clear Bitty's threshold but also clear a higher threshold elsewhere, you should almost always choose the higher-tier lender. Better-capitalized MCA funders like [Kapitus](/articles/kapitus-review), [CAN Capital](/articles/can-capital-review), and others price meaningfully lower than Bitty because their underwriting pools are less risky.

<h2 id="fees-and-true-cost">Fees and True Cost</h2>

The headline factor rate is not the whole cost. Bitty's fee structure, like most MCA funders, includes multiple layers that add up:

- **Origination fee.** Typically $195 to $495 depending on deal size. Deducted from the advance amount, so the net funded amount is lower than the headline advance.
- **Wire or ACH fee.** $15 to $50 per transaction. Accumulates across the life of the deal since most MCAs debit daily.
- **Renewal fees.** If Bitty converts your account at renewal, expect another origination fee on the new advance.
- **NSF fees.** $35 to $50 per rejected debit. NSF activity also damages Bitty's underwriting view of the account, which can push any renewal pricing higher.
- **Default or acceleration terms.** If you default, the remaining factor-rate balance may accelerate to become immediately due. Default interest rates in some contracts can be extreme.

Run a full example. On a $15,000 advance at 1.40 factor rate, 6-month term:

- Headline advance: $15,000
- Origination fee: $295
- Net funded: $14,705
- Total repayment: $21,000
- Total cost including origination: $6,295
- Daily payment (126 business days): approximately $166.67
- Effective APR: roughly 190 to 230 percent depending on repayment pace

Now compare that to a $15,000 SBA microloan at 11 percent APR, 5-year term: total interest cost around $4,575 over 5 years, with a much gentler monthly payment. The comparison only works if you qualify for the microloan, but the math is stark. <h2 id="red-flags-customer-reviews">Red Flags and Customer Reviews</h2>

Public reviews of Bitty Advance trend toward two poles: positive reviews from borrowers who got fast funding that their bank declined, and negative reviews from borrowers for whom the factor rate created cash flow pressure that escalated into default or litigation. Read both poles carefully because they represent different borrower profiles, not contradictory reports on the same product.

Sources to triangulate:

- **Better Business Bureau.** Search the exact legal entity on [bbb.org](https://www.bbb.org). Note accreditation status, complaint count over the past three years, and whether complaints cluster around specific themes (repayment enforcement, disclosure issues, renewal pressure). A pattern is signal.
- **Trustpilot and Google Reviews.** Filter to one and two star reviews. Look for specifics: dollar amounts, advance sizes, factor rates disclosed. Identical phrasing across five-star reviews suggests template management.
- **Reddit.** Based on public reviews on Reddit in r/smallbusiness and r/Entrepreneur, Bitty threads typically include discussion of the small-advance-with-high-factor structure. Read the threads in full for context.
- **Court records.** Run a search on [PACER](https://www.pacer.gov/) and state court records for Bitty Advance as a plaintiff. Litigation volume tells you about default patterns and enforcement aggressiveness.

Watch for these specific red flags common to the MCA industry and relevant to smaller-deal funders:

- **Renewal pressure.** If a funder calls you weekly to offer a renewal or top-up before you are ready, that is a sign the funder is more focused on origination than on your success.
- **Confession of judgment.** See our [MCA confession of judgment](/articles/mca-confession-of-judgment) explainer. Confirm what is in your specific contract.
- **Personal guarantee scope.** Personal guarantees are standard in MCA contracts. The question is scope: does it apply only to fraud or misrepresentation, or is it a general-recourse personal guarantee?
- **Default acceleration.** Read the default and acceleration clause carefully. Some contracts allow the funder to accelerate the full factor-rate balance on a single missed debit.

Before signing a Bitty Advance deal at 1.40+ factor rate, run through the alternative ladder:

- **SBA microloans.** Up to $50,000 through nonprofit intermediary lenders at 8 to 13 percent APR. Application is heavier than an MCA but the cost savings are enormous. Start with the [SBA microloan directory](https://www.sba.gov/funding-programs/loans/microloans).
- **CDFI funding.** Community Development Financial Institutions lend to underserved businesses and smaller operators who cannot qualify for bank credit. Rates are higher than SBA but far lower than MCA. Find your local CDFI through the [Treasury CDFI Fund locator](https://www.cdfifund.gov/).
- **Revenue-based financing.** A structurally similar product to MCA but with better disclosure and often lower effective APR. Our [revenue-based financing](/articles/revenue-based-financing) guide covers the mechanics.
- **Invoice factoring.** For B2B businesses with receivables, factoring is often dramatically cheaper than MCA. See [invoice factoring guide](/articles/invoice-factoring-guide).
- **Other MCA funders.** If an MCA is genuinely the right product for your situation, compare Bitty against funders that may offer better terms if you can qualify: [Fora Financial](/articles/fora-financial-review), [CAN Capital](/articles/can-capital-review), [Credibly](/articles/credibly-review), [National Funding](/articles/national-funding-review), and [Kapitus](/articles/kapitus-review).
- **DIY bootstrap and cost cuts.** Before any MCA, see our [fix cashflow before MCA](/articles/fix-cashflow-before-mca) guide. Often the cash pressure that drove the MCA search can be addressed operationally for far less than $6,000 in MCA fees.
