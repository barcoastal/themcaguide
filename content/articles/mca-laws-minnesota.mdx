Minnesota has a strong consumer protection focus, and while the state does not have legislation specifically targeting merchant cash advances, its regulatory framework provides meaningful protections for business owners. The Minnesota Department of Commerce and the state's broad consumer fraud laws create an environment where predatory MCA practices can be challenged.

This guide explains how Minnesota law applies to MCAs and what steps you can take to protect your business.

<h2 id="current-mca-regulations">Current MCA Regulations in Minnesota</h2>

Minnesota does not classify merchant cash advances as loans. Because MCAs are structured as purchases of future receivables, they fall outside the state's traditional lending regulations, including interest rate caps and lender licensing requirements.

There is no Minnesota law requiring MCA companies to provide standardized disclosures such as an estimated APR or total cost of financing. However, the Minnesota Department of Commerce actively monitors commercial financing products and has shown a willingness to address practices that harm small businesses.

<h3 id="department-of-commerce">Department of Commerce Oversight</h3>

The Minnesota Department of Commerce regulates financial services companies in the state and has broad authority to investigate unfair or deceptive practices. While the Department does not license MCA companies specifically, it can take action against companies that operate in ways that violate Minnesota law. The Department also accepts complaints from business owners and can refer cases to the Attorney General for enforcement.

<h2 id="confession-of-judgment-rules">Confession of Judgment Rules</h2>

Minnesota provides meaningful protections against confessions of judgment:

- Minnesota law restricts the use of cognovit notes (confessions of judgment) and requires strict procedural compliance before a COJ can be entered
- A COJ must be based on a written instrument that clearly authorizes the entry of judgment
- Minnesota courts can vacate a COJ if the debtor demonstrates that it was obtained through fraud, duress, or lack of proper authorization
- Out-of-state COJ judgments must be domesticated through Minnesota courts, where the debtor has the opportunity to raise defenses

These protections mean that an MCA company cannot simply file a COJ in another state and automatically enforce it in Minnesota. You have the right to challenge the judgment in Minnesota courts.

<h2 id="ucc-filing-rules">UCC Filing Rules</h2>

MCA companies operating in Minnesota file UCC-1 financing statements with the Minnesota Secretary of State. These filings put other creditors on notice that the MCA company has a claim on your future receivables.

What you need to know about UCC filings in Minnesota:

- You can search for UCC filings against your business through the [Minnesota Secretary of State](https://www.sos.state.mn.us/) online portal
- UCC filings in Minnesota are effective for five years from the date of filing
- After you pay off an MCA, the funder is required to file a UCC-3 termination statement within 20 business days of receiving your written demand
- If the funder fails to terminate the filing, you may pursue damages under Minnesota's Uniform Commercial Code provisions

Stacked UCC filings from multiple MCA companies will hurt your chances of getting approved for traditional bank financing. Review your filings before applying for any new credit.

<h2 id="consumer-protection-laws">Consumer Protection Laws That Apply</h2>

Minnesota has robust consumer protection laws that can apply to MCA transactions.

<h3 id="consumer-fraud-act">Minnesota Consumer Fraud Act</h3>

The Minnesota Consumer Fraud Act (Minn. Stat. 325F.69) prohibits fraud, misrepresentation, and deceptive practices in connection with the sale of merchandise or services. While MCAs are not traditional consumer products, Minnesota courts have applied the Consumer Fraud Act in commercial contexts where the conduct involves deception or material misrepresentation.

Protections under the Act include:

- Prohibition against making false or misleading statements about the cost or terms of financing
- Prohibition against omitting material facts that a business owner would need to make an informed decision
- Private right of action allowing business owners to sue for damages
- Potential recovery of attorney's fees in successful cases

<h3 id="deceptive-trade-practices">Deceptive Trade Practices Act</h3>

The Minnesota Deceptive Trade Practices Act (Minn. Stat. 325D.44) provides additional protections against false advertising, misleading representations, and other deceptive conduct. This statute can be used alongside the Consumer Fraud Act to build a comprehensive case against a predatory MCA company.

<h2 id="recent-legislation">Recent Legislation and Court Cases</h2>

Minnesota's approach to MCA regulation has evolved in recent years:

- **Consumer protection focus.** The Minnesota Attorney General's office has prioritized protecting small businesses from predatory financial products, including issuing guidance on recognizing deceptive financing offers.
- **No MCA-specific bills.** Minnesota has not introduced legislation specifically targeting MCAs, but the state has participated in national conversations about commercial financing regulation.
- **Court decisions.** Minnesota courts have handled MCA disputes on a case-by-case basis, with outcomes depending on the specific contract terms and whether the MCA company engaged in deceptive practices.
- **Multi-state coordination.** Minnesota has worked with other states and federal agencies to monitor trends in the MCA industry and share information about companies that generate complaints.

<h2 id="what-business-owners-should-do">What Minnesota Business Owners Should Do</h2>

If you are considering an MCA in Minnesota or already have one, take these steps:

1. **Ask for full cost disclosure.** Request the total repayment amount, total cost of financing, estimated APR, and payment schedule in writing. If the MCA company will not provide this information clearly, consider it a warning sign.
2. **Check your UCC filings.** Search the [Minnesota Secretary of State](https://www.sos.state.mn.us/) database to see what liens exist on your business. Clean up any outdated filings from MCAs you have already paid off.
3. **Understand the reconciliation terms.** A legitimate MCA adjusts your daily or weekly payment based on your actual sales. If your payments are fixed regardless of revenue, the product may be structured as a loan, which could make it subject to Minnesota lending laws.
4. **Refuse to sign a confession of judgment.** Minnesota restricts COJs, but the best protection is to avoid agreeing to one in the first place. Ask for the clause to be removed before signing.
5. **Report problems.** File complaints with the [Minnesota Attorney General](https://www.ag.state.mn.us/) and the [Minnesota Department of Commerce](https://mn.gov/commerce/) if you encounter deceptive or predatory conduct.
