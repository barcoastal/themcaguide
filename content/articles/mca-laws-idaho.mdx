Idaho is known as a business-friendly state with minimal financial regulation. There are no state laws that specifically address merchant cash advances. For Idaho business owners considering an MCA, this means fewer built-in protections compared to states like California or New York.

This guide explains what Idaho law does and does not cover when it comes to MCAs, and what steps you can take to protect yourself.

<h2 id="current-mca-regulations">Current MCA Regulations in Idaho</h2>

Idaho does not have any laws or regulations that specifically govern merchant cash advances. The Idaho Department of Finance oversees banking, lending, and securities in the state, but MCAs fall outside its regulatory scope because they are structured as purchases of future receivables rather than loans.

This means MCA companies operating in Idaho are not required to obtain a state license, disclose an APR, or follow any standardized disclosure format when offering advances to Idaho businesses. The terms of your MCA agreement are governed almost entirely by the contract you sign.

<h3 id="idaho-credit-code">Idaho Credit Code</h3>

Idaho's Credit Code (Title 28, Chapter 41 of the Idaho Code) regulates consumer credit transactions and sets usury limits for consumer loans. However, because MCAs are classified as commercial transactions and not loans, the Credit Code does not apply. Idaho does not impose usury caps on commercial transactions, which means there is no legal ceiling on the effective cost of an MCA in the state.

<h2 id="confession-of-judgment-rules">Confession of Judgment Rules</h2>

Idaho does not have a specific statute that prohibits confessions of judgment in commercial transactions. This means that if you sign an MCA agreement containing a COJ clause, the MCA company may be able to use it to obtain a judgment against your business without a trial.

However, Idaho courts do require that COJ clauses be entered into knowingly and voluntarily. If you can show that you did not understand what you were signing, or that the clause was buried in fine print, you may have grounds to challenge the judgment.

Key points for Idaho business owners:

- Read every page of your MCA agreement before signing
- Look specifically for confession of judgment language
- If you find a COJ clause, ask the MCA company to remove it or consult an attorney before proceeding
- If a COJ judgment is entered against you, contact an Idaho attorney immediately to explore your options

<h2 id="ucc-filing-rules">UCC Filing Rules</h2>

MCA companies file UCC-1 financing statements with the Idaho Secretary of State to establish their claim on your future receivables. These filings are public records and can affect your ability to obtain other financing.

What you need to know about UCC filings in Idaho:

- You can search for UCC filings against your business through the [Idaho Secretary of State](https://sos.idaho.gov/) website
- UCC filings in Idaho are effective for five years from the date of filing
- When you pay off an MCA, the funder is required to file a UCC-3 termination statement within 20 days of receiving your written request
- If the funder fails to file a termination, you may have remedies under Idaho's version of the Uniform Commercial Code (Title 28, Chapter 9)

<h2 id="consumer-protection-laws">Consumer Protection Laws That Apply</h2>

While Idaho lacks MCA-specific regulation, the state's general consumer protection laws may offer some protection.

<h3 id="icpa">Idaho Consumer Protection Act</h3>

The Idaho Consumer Protection Act (Idaho Code Section 48-601 et seq.) prohibits unfair or deceptive acts or practices in trade or commerce. While this law is primarily designed for consumer transactions, Idaho courts have applied it to business-to-business transactions in certain circumstances.

If an MCA company made false representations about the cost, terms, or nature of your advance, the Consumer Protection Act may provide a basis for legal action. The Idaho Attorney General's office enforces this statute and accepts complaints.

<h3 id="no-disclosure-requirements">No Disclosure Requirements</h3>

Unlike California and New York, Idaho does not require MCA companies to provide standardized disclosures about APR, total cost, or payment terms. This makes it especially important for Idaho business owners to do their own due diligence before signing an MCA agreement.

<h2 id="recent-legislation">Recent Legislation and Court Cases</h2>

As of early 2026, Idaho has not introduced or passed any legislation specifically targeting MCA regulation. The state legislature has not signaled any plans to adopt disclosure requirements or licensing rules for MCA providers.

- **No pending MCA bills.** Idaho's legislature has not taken up MCA-specific legislation in recent sessions.
- **Federal developments.** Idaho businesses may benefit from any future federal regulation of MCAs, which has been discussed at the congressional level but has not yet materialized.
- **Industry trends.** As more states adopt MCA disclosure laws, there may be increasing pressure on Idaho to follow suit, but no timeline has been established.

<h2 id="what-business-owners-should-do">What Idaho Business Owners Should Do</h2>

Without state-level MCA protections, Idaho business owners need to be proactive:

1. **Calculate the true cost yourself.** Since MCA companies in Idaho are not required to disclose an APR, convert the factor rate and repayment terms into an annualized cost. A factor rate of 1.40 on a 6-month advance translates to an effective APR of roughly 80% or more.
2. **Refuse COJ clauses.** Ask the MCA company to remove any confession of judgment language from the agreement. If they refuse, consider whether the advance is worth the risk.
3. **Check your UCC filings.** Search the [Idaho Secretary of State](https://sos.idaho.gov/) website for any existing liens on your business before taking on additional MCA debt.
4. **Get legal review.** Have an Idaho business attorney review the MCA agreement before you sign. The cost of a legal review is small compared to the potential cost of a bad MCA deal.
5. **Explore alternatives first.** Contact the [Idaho Small Business Development Center](https://idahosbdc.org/) for free counseling on financing options that may be less expensive than an MCA.
