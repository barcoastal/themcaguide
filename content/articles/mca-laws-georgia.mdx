Georgia is one of the biggest MCA markets in the southeastern United States, driven by a large and growing small business population, particularly in the Atlanta metro area. However, the state has limited regulation specific to merchant cash advances, which means Georgia business owners need to be especially careful when considering an MCA.

This guide covers the current state of MCA law in Georgia and what you can do to protect yourself.

<h2 id="current-mca-regulations">Current MCA Regulations in Georgia</h2>

Georgia does not have any laws specifically regulating merchant cash advances. Like many states, Georgia treats MCAs as commercial transactions rather than loans, which places them outside the scope of the Georgia Industrial Loan Act and other lending regulations.

The practical implications:

- MCA companies do not need a Georgia lending license to offer MCAs
- Georgia's usury laws (which limit interest to 5% per month, or 60% annually, for commercial loans) do not apply to MCAs
- There are no state-mandated disclosure requirements for MCAs
- The Georgia Department of Banking and Finance does not directly oversee MCA transactions

This regulatory gap means that Georgia business owners lack the protections available in states like California, New York, and Illinois. You are largely relying on general contract law and federal protections.

<h2 id="confession-of-judgment-rules">Confession of Judgment Rules</h2>

Georgia provides moderate protection against confessions of judgment. Under Georgia law (O.C.G.A. Section 9-12-15 et seq.):

- Confessions of judgment are permitted in Georgia but must meet specific statutory requirements
- The confession must be in writing and signed by the debtor
- Georgia courts have the authority to review COJs and can refuse to enter judgment if the COJ was improperly obtained
- The debtor has the right to open default and present defenses if they can show a meritorious defense exists

While Georgia does not ban COJs outright like Texas, the procedural requirements provide some protection. If a COJ is filed against you in Georgia, you have legal options to challenge it.

For COJ judgments obtained in other states, Georgia requires domestication through the Uniform Enforcement of Foreign Judgments Act. This process gives you an opportunity to challenge the judgment before it can be enforced against your Georgia assets.

<h2 id="ucc-filing-rules">UCC Filing Rules</h2>

MCA companies file UCC-1 financing statements with the Georgia Superior Court Clerks' Cooperative Authority. These filings create a public lien on your business assets.

Key information for Georgia business owners:

- Search for UCC filings through the [Georgia Superior Court Clerks' Cooperative Authority](https://www.gsccca.org/) website
- Georgia UCC filings are effective for five years
- After satisfying your MCA, demand a UCC-3 termination in writing. The funder has 20 days to comply.
- Georgia's UCC Article 9 (O.C.G.A. Title 11, Article 9) provides remedies for wrongful failure to terminate a filing

<h2 id="consumer-protection-laws">Consumer Protection Laws That Apply</h2>

<h3 id="fbpa">Georgia Fair Business Practices Act (FBPA)</h3>

The FBPA (O.C.G.A. Section 10-1-390 et seq.) is Georgia's primary consumer protection law. It prohibits unfair or deceptive acts or practices in trade or commerce. While primarily designed for consumer transactions, certain provisions can apply to business-to-business transactions where deceptive practices are involved.

If an MCA company engaged in deceptive conduct. such as misrepresenting costs, hiding fees, or making false promises. the FBPA may provide a legal remedy. However, the FBPA's applicability to purely commercial MCA transactions can be limited, so consult an attorney about your specific situation.

<h3 id="common-law-remedies">Common Law Remedies</h3>

Georgia courts apply standard contract law principles that can help in MCA disputes:

- **Fraud.** If the MCA company made material misrepresentations to induce you to sign the agreement.
- **Unconscionability.** If the MCA terms are so one-sided that they "shock the conscience" of the court.
- **Duress.** If you were improperly pressured into signing the agreement.
- **Breach of contract.** If the MCA company failed to perform reconciliation or violated other terms of the agreement.

<h2 id="recent-developments">Recent Developments</h2>

Georgia has not passed MCA-specific legislation, but several developments are relevant:

- **Growing MCA activity.** Atlanta's position as a major business hub has made Georgia one of the top MCA markets in the Southeast. Increased activity has brought more complaints and regulatory attention.
- **Legislative awareness.** Georgia lawmakers have begun discussing the need for commercial financing regulation, following the lead of states like California and New York.
- **Federal oversight.** The FTC's increased focus on deceptive commercial financing practices impacts MCA companies operating in Georgia.
- **Court scrutiny.** Georgia courts have examined MCA agreements and some judges have expressed concern about the lack of transparency in MCA pricing.
- **Attorney General investigations.** The Georgia Attorney General's Consumer Protection Division has received complaints about MCA practices and monitors the industry.

<h2 id="what-business-owners-should-do">What Georgia Business Owners Should Do</h2>

Given the limited regulatory protections in Georgia, proactive self-protection is essential:

1. **Calculate the true cost.** Since Georgia does not require APR disclosures, ask the MCA company for the total payback amount and the expected term. Calculate the effective annual cost yourself, or ask your accountant to do it.
2. **Read the entire agreement.** Pay attention to the personal guarantee, COJ clause, default provisions, choice-of-law clause, and reconciliation terms.
3. **Check UCC filings.** Search the [GSCCCA](https://www.gsccca.org/) website for existing liens against your business before taking on MCA debt.
4. **Get legal advice before signing.** A Georgia business attorney can identify problematic terms and may be able to negotiate better conditions.
5. **Avoid stacking.** Without regulatory guardrails, taking multiple MCAs simultaneously is especially dangerous in Georgia. The combined daily payments can quickly become unsustainable.
6. **Document everything.** Keep records of all payments, communications, and any promises made by the MCA company. If reconciliation was promised but not performed, document this.
7. **Explore alternatives.** Contact the [SBA Georgia District Office](https://www.sba.gov/district/georgia) or [Georgia SBDC](https://www.georgiasbdc.org/) for lower-cost financing options.
